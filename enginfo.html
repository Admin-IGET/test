Question: Why doesn’t UvikOS work well with antivirus software?

Answer: Antiviruses like AVG or Avast need some time to scan files and determine whether they are safe. In UvikOS, some files may be flagged as IDP.Generic, but that only means the software looks like malware, not that it actually is. While the antivirus is scanning, it may pause UvikOS (or parts of it) due to how it is implemented.

Question: Why is UvikOS being flagged as a virus?

Answer: Windows Defender doesn't actually scan your files immediately, and if you download a file that seems suspicious, it doesn’t let you download or open it. Proper antiviruses that actually scan your files may say that some files have the IDP.Generic infection, which means it LOOKS like malware, not that it IS malware.

Question: Why does UvikOS look like an IDP.Generic infection to some antiviruses?

Answer: UvikOS is often flagged as IDP.Generic mainly because of its file format (.ps1) and code that accesses system DLLs, such as dwmapi.dll (for window customization) or gdi32.dll (for bitmap handling), etc. These actions may be considered suspicious by antiviruses like AVG or Avast, which then display a warning.

Question: Why did my UvikOS randomly crash and won’t start again?

Answer: This can happen when UvikOS tries to access certain files (e.g., C:\edit\uvik.png when changing an icon) while AVG or Avast is active. The antivirus may interpret this as an attempt to modify files through mspaint, which is proc.WaitForExit-ed.

You can fix these inconveniences by putting the whole "C:\apps\" folder (the folder where UvikOS is installed and nothing else) to the exceptions list in your antivirus.
